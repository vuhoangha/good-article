            Whiteknight a year ago

            It's important to understand that these database types don't just have best-cases, but using the wrong database for the wrong type of work load can lead to pathological worst-cases. I've been in organizations that use a single DB (usually SQL) for everything, and some products make good use of it and other products are abysmal: Development takes longer, performance is terrible, there are more bugs and issues, customers always unhappy, etc. And you hear people say "we're going to spend months trying to research the absolute best alternative", all the while staying on the pathological worst-case and accruing debt both technical and in customer good will. Having lots of DB options available, getting a good sense of the strengths and weaknesses of each, and having the flexibility to choose one that is good enough (if not absolute best) for a particular workload is a huge benefit for developers and solution architects.

                    this quote: "databases are built for a purpose and matching the use case with the database will help you write high-performance, scalable, and more functional applications faster."

                    turns out that your products are so good that people do end up using them for a different purpose. Take Amazon Redshift. I remember when Amazon Redshift was launched, a question came from the audience, if you can use Redshift as an OLTP database, even though it's OLAP. Turns out using Redshift in an OLTP scenario is one of the major use cases, to build analytical applications.

                    We are one of those use cases, we've built an analytical app on top of Redshift. The OLTP use case stretches Redshift once you start putting a serious number of users on it. Even with the best WLM configuration.

                    To solve for that, we've used a combination of Amazon RDS, Amazon Redshift and dblink plus Lambda and Elasticsearch. Detailed write-up on how we did it here:

                    https://www.intermix.io/blo...

                    We called that "Have Your Postgres Cake with Amazon Redshift and eat it, too." The approach has allowed us to get the best of both worlds - Redshift's analytical capabilities with the speed of transactional queries via Amazon RDS

                                    Dragan Babovic 10 months ago

                                    The most important thing is to model data. The next most important thing is to model data properly. Once that is done the right product can be chosen. Unfortunately, very often the database design is done for developer's convenience in that particular moment.Sometimes they are pressured by deadlines, sometimes they do believe that it is the right way to do it. Then the wrong model and design cast a shadow on the underlying technology.

                                    The relational databases are around for so long, that the amount of time and effort to master them may seem disheartening to young developers, and they simply look for something else that is easier to master. As they mature they will take more senior positions and so they will phase out older technologies including relational databases. It is this human factor that will render that technology obsolete. So no, relational databases are not well, and they won't be alive for long (a decade or so). We have to adjust to that reality.
